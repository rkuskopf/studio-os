# Alt Cosmetics — Branding Terminology Reference

A working glossary of terms used in the identity development process. Written for clarity, not academia. Organised from broad to specific.

---

## Part 1 — Logo: what the word actually covers

**Logo** is an umbrella term. In everyday speech it means "the visual mark of a brand" — but it doesn't specify what form that mark takes. When designers say "logo" they usually mean the whole identity system, or at minimum the primary mark. The term is imprecise on its own, which is why the sub-categories below exist.

**Wordmark** — the brand name set in a specific typeface, sometimes customised or hand-drawn. The letterforms *are* the mark. Coca-Cola, Google, FedEx. No icon, no symbol — just the name. A wordmark can be a logo on its own.

**Lettermark** — initials only, set typographically. LV (Louis Vuitton), YSL, IBM. The distinction from a wordmark is that only initials are used, not the full name. Related to monogram (see below).

**Logomark / Symbol / Icon** — the non-typographic element: a shape, symbol, or abstract form that represents the brand without spelling out the name. The Nike swoosh, the Apple apple, the Lacoste crocodile. This is what people usually mean when they say "logo mark" — the icon part, separate from the text.

**Combination mark** — a logomark *plus* a wordmark used together as a single unit. Most brand identities start here because the icon alone doesn't yet have recognition. Over time the icon may be used solo (Nike no longer needs the word "Nike" next to the swoosh).

**Emblem** — a mark where the text is integrated *inside* or *around* the icon — the text and symbol can't be separated cleanly. Think crests, seals, badges. Different from a combination mark where the icon and wordmark sit alongside each other but remain visually independent.

## Part 2 — Mark types: the specific forms

These are the distinct structures a logo mark can take. All of them are types of logomark or emblem.

**Monogram** — a design composed of two or more letters, usually initials, overlaid or interlocked. The letters aren't simply placed next to each other — they share strokes, overlap, or weave together to form a single composed unit. LV (Louis Vuitton) is a monogram. The Mauclot reference in Direction B is a monogram sheet. Historically used on personal possessions (stationery, silverware, linen) to mark ownership.

**Cipher** — a specific type of monogram where the letters are ornately interlocked into a highly decorative single form. The distinction is degree: a monogram can be simple, a cipher is always elaborate. Royal ciphers (EIIR — Elizabeth II Regina) are the classic example. The word implies a kind of coded or compressed identity.

**Cartouche** — an oval or oblong frame (sometimes rectangular with rounded ends) that encloses text or symbols. Originates in Egyptian hieroglyphics, where a cartouche enclosed the names of pharaohs. Used widely in architectural ornament, cartography, heraldry, and printed books to frame important information with decorative authority. Direction C's SRA SRA reference is a cartouche format: letters arranged inside a structured oval ring.

**Seal** — historically, a device pressed into wax or embossed on paper to authenticate a document. The impression itself. Seals can take many forms (circular, oval, heraldic), but the defining quality is the idea of official authentication — weight, authority, permanence. The format typically shows text around a perimeter ring enclosing a central device or image. Companies still use "corporate seal" in legal contexts.

**Signet** — a personal seal, usually set in a ring. Smaller, more intimate than an institutional seal. Used to mark personal correspondence. The signet ring is the object; the signet is the design pressed into it. The word carries aristocratic and personal authority.

**Crest** — in heraldry, technically refers only to the element sitting *on top* of a helmet in a coat of arms (see below). In common use, "crest" is used loosely to mean any emblem or badge with a formal, heraldic-adjacent quality. Sports teams, schools, clubs, and institutions use "crest" to describe their marks — usually meaning a structured emblem with multiple elements. The word implies heritage, earned status, and institutional authority.

**Badge** — a mark that identifies membership, achievement, or affiliation. Simpler and more direct than a crest. Badges are worn, displayed, applied. In design, a badge-style mark is usually an enclosed self-contained form — often circular, oval, or shield-shaped — with elements arranged inside. Less formal than a seal, more direct than a crest.

**Device** — the older, more elegant term for what we'd now call a logomark or symbol. Used in historical printing, heraldry, and Renaissance visual culture. A printer's device was the distinctive mark a printing house placed in books. A personal device was a symbolic image (sometimes with a motto) representing an individual — the forerunner of the modern brand mark. The word is underused now, but carries exactly the right register for what ALT. is trying to make.

## Part 3 — Surface application terms

These describe how a mark is applied to a physical surface. For ALT. Cosmetics, these are directly relevant to the tube and packaging.

**Engraving** — cutting a design *into* a hard surface (metal, stone, glass, wood) using a tool. The mark sits below the surface plane. Historically used for printing plates, jewellery, awards. On packaging, machine engraving or laser engraving cuts the mark into the material directly.

**Intaglio** — Italian for "carved" or "cut." In printmaking, an intaglio plate is one where the image is incised *below* the surface — ink sits in the recessed lines and is transferred under pressure. Intaglio is the technique; engraving is one method of producing it. The aesthetic quality — precise, incised, worn-in — is exactly Direction A for ALT. As a descriptor for a mark's register: "intaglio quality" means it looks carved, incised, pressed into the material rather than sitting on top.

**Debossing** — pressing a die into a surface to create a *recessed* (sunken) impression. The mark sits *below* the surface. The opposite of embossing. On packaging, debossed marks have a quiet, restrained quality — the mark reads by shadow and depth, not colour. Extremely relevant for ALT's chrome tube direction.

**Embossing** — pressing a die into a surface to create a *raised* impression. The mark sits *above* the surface. The opposite of debossing. Embossed marks are tactile and visible from an angle. More conventional on cosmetic packaging than debossing.

**Hot stamping** (also: foil stamping, hot foiling) — a process where metallic or pigment foil is transferred to a surface using heat and pressure via a metal die. Used to apply gold, silver, holographic, or chrome foil finishes. The result looks applied rather than incised. On ALT's chrome tube, hot stamping could create a mark in a contrasting or tone-on-tone foil. The die for hot stamping can also create a simultaneous emboss.

**Relief** — a raised design. In sculpture, relief describes figures that project from a flat background. In print and packaging, "relief printing" or a "relief mark" means the design stands proud of the surface. Embossing is a relief process. The opposite of intaglio.

## Part 4 — Historical institutional identity terms

**Coat of arms** — the full heraldic composition used by a family, institution, or nation. Includes: shield (the central element, bearing the main devices), crest (the element above the helmet), helmet/mantling (decorative drapery), supporters (figures flanking the shield), motto (text below). The whole thing together is a "coat of arms." The shield alone is the "arms." In common use, "coat of arms" has come to mean any formal institutional mark with historical gravitas.

**Heraldry** — the system, practice, and language of designing, displaying, and regulating coats of arms. Heraldry developed in 12th-century Europe as a practical system for identifying knights in battle (they were covered in armour). Every element has specific meaning and terminology (gules = red, or = gold, a chevron is a specific shape, etc.). The visual language of heraldry — precision, rules, symbolic density — directly informs the "Victorian / Medieval" register in Direction B.

**Armorial bearings** — a more formal term for a coat of arms. Used in legal and genealogical contexts.

**Sigil** — historically, a symbolic seal or sign used in magic and occult practice, each representing a specific spirit or concept. In contemporary design/culture, "sigil" has been reclaimed to mean any compressed, abstracted mark that carries intent or identity. It's now used loosely in brand design to describe abstract marks with a quasi-mystical or archaeological register. The word is useful for Direction A.

**Colophon** — in historical printing, a printer's mark placed at the end of a book, identifying the printer, date, and place of production. Often distinctive and decorative — the forerunner of the modern brand mark on packaging. The colophon of Aldus Manutius (the anchor and dolphin) is one of the most famous historical examples of what we'd now call a logomark.

**Impresa** (plural: imprese) — a Renaissance personal device: a symbolic image combined with a motto, expressing an individual's character, ambition, or values. Used by nobles and rulers. The forerunner of the modern brand identity system. Different from a coat of arms in that it was personal and expressive rather than genealogically inherited.

## Part 5 — What ALT. is actually making, and what to call it

Based on the directions in development, the most accurate descriptors for what ALT. Cosmetics is trying to make are:

**What it is structurally:**
- A **brand mark** — the primary graphic device that will identify the brand on packaging and collateral
- Specifically, likely a **lettermark** or **monogram** (initials-based: A, L, T)
- Possibly housed within a **cartouche** (Direction C) or presented as a standalone incised/carved form (Directions A and B)

**What it should feel like on the surface:**
- **Debossed** or **engraved** — incised into the chrome tube, not sitting on top of it
- **Intaglio in register** — the aesthetic quality of being carved, worn, pressed in

**What it's closest to historically:**
- A **signet** (personal, intimate, authoritative)
- A **device** (a refined historical mark representing the brand, not a family)
- A **cipher** (if Direction B develops into interlocked decorative letterforms)
- A **cartouche** (if Direction C develops into letters-within-a-frame)

**Terms that work in client communication:**
- "Mark" — neutral, professional, works across all three directions
- "Brand mark" — same, slightly more formal
- "Device" — carries the right historical register without being inaccessible
- "Monogram" — accurate if the mark is initials-based, and Anh will understand it immediately
- "Signet" — useful when describing the feel/application quality (the tube is like a signet ring for the brand)

**Terms to use carefully:**
- "Logo" — fine as shorthand but imprecise; use "mark" or "brand mark" for clarity
- "Crest" — implies heraldic complexity; only use if Direction C develops that way
- "Seal" — implies institutional officialness; useful as a register descriptor, less useful as a primary term
- "Coat of arms" — too heraldic; not this project unless deliberately referencing that tradition
