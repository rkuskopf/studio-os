# Humanising AI Communication
## A guide to making synthetic writing feel authored, not generated

---

## The Problem

The most persistent failure of AI-generated writing is not inaccuracy. It is **placelessness** — language that could have been written about anything, by anyone, for no one in particular.

George Orwell diagnosed this condition in 1946, before AI existed. The writer, he argued, reaches for familiar phrases rather than thinking freshly, and the result is prose that "anaesthetises" rather than communicates. The solution he prescribed remains the most practical instruction set for training an agent to write like a person:

- Never use a long word where a short one will do
- Never use a metaphor you have seen in print before
- Never use the passive where the active will do
- If it is possible to cut a word, cut it

AI doesn't do this by default. It does the opposite — it reaches for the most statistically common version of every idea and calls it writing.

---

## The Literary Foundation

Before encoding rules into prompts, understand where the rules come from. These are the primary references:

**Italo Calvino — *Six Memos for the Next Millennium* (Literature / Philosophy of Language)**
Six lectures on the qualities that make writing endure. The two most relevant:
- *Lightness*: carry weight without showing the effort. The reader should do some of the work. The moment writing explains its own meaning, it has failed.
- *Exactitude*: every word must be load-bearing. Decoration is a symptom of uncertainty about what you are actually trying to say.

**George Orwell — "Politics and the English Language" (Essay / Political Philosophy)**
A 1946 essay that remains the sharpest diagnosis of bad writing. Orwell identified that lazy language is not just an aesthetic problem — it is an intellectual one. Vague writing produces vague thinking. Read this first. Twenty minutes.

**Ernest Hemingway — The Iceberg Theory (Literary Theory)**
The dignity of movement comes from what is left out. Seven eighths below the surface. Applied to AI: if you have said it, cut it. The reader should feel what is not written.

**William Strunk & E.B. White — *The Elements of Style* (Style / Grammar)**
"Omit needless words." Not a style preference — a discipline. One sitting.

**Roland Barthes — "The Death of the Author" (Literary Theory / Semiotics)**
Writing that performs its own intelligence is the writer getting in the way. The instruction: disappear into the subject.

**Anton Chekhov — The Gun Principle (Dramatic Theory)**
If you show a gun in the first act it must fire in the third. Every detail load-bearing, nothing decorative. Applied to writing: never include a detail that does not do work.

## On Analogical Reasoning

The vocabulary that sharpens AI writing does not come from writing itself. It comes from disciplines that have spent longer and thought more rigorously about the same underlying problems.

This is **analogical reasoning** — looking laterally across fields for concepts that have already solved a version of the problem at hand, then borrowing the reasoning rather than the vocabulary. The distinction matters. Borrowing vocabulary produces an agent that reaches for the same borrowed terms regardless of context. Borrowing reasoning produces an agent that knows how to think.

When a piece of writing feels placeless — generic, untethered, applicable to anything — the relevant school of thought is not copywriting. It is **critical theory**, specifically the branch concerned with how meaning is produced in relation to context, culture, and place. Architecture, phenomenology, and Continental philosophy have all grappled with this problem at depth. Heidegger on dwelling. Bachelard on the poetics of space. Frampton on critical regionalism. None of these are about writing. All of them are about the same question: what does it mean for something to belong where it is?

The instruction to an agent is not to use the vocabulary of these disciplines. It is to consult the school of thought most relevant to the problem, understand its core reasoning, and apply that reasoning to the task. The output should feel as though the agent has thought, not as though it has retrieved.

**The method:**
1. Identify the quality that is missing from the writing — not as an aesthetic preference but as an intellectual problem.
2. Ask which discipline has thought most carefully about that problem.
3. Borrow its reasoning. Apply it. Leave its terminology behind.

## Worked Example: From Generated to Written

### The Cold Outreach Emails

Here is what AI produced when asked to write cold outreach for KSPF:

> *"I've been following [Agency Name]'s work — specifically the [mention a recent project they did] — and love the aesthetic direction you take. I'm a Senior UX Researcher and Designer (5+ years) at KSPF Studio..."*

And:

> *"I noticed you're currently running on a standard Shopify framework. While it works, the site feels a bit 'templated' compared to the premium nature of your products (especially the $250 workshops)."*

**What is wrong with these:**

**1. They lead with credentials instead of observation.**
"I'm a Senior UX Researcher and Designer (5+ years)" — the reader doesn't care yet. The writer has not earned the right to introduce themselves because they have not shown they know anything about the reader. The iceberg is missing. There is nothing below the surface.

**2. They name the value instead of demonstrating it.**
"The site feels a bit templated compared to the premium nature of your products" — this is telling someone what their problem is before they have agreed they have it. It sounds like a sales script because it is one.

**3. They use the language of categories, not people.**
"High-taste but low-performance brands." "Aesop-adjacent." "CFO-approved hunting strategy." This is the AI talking to itself using vocabulary it generated during the strategy phase. None of it sounds like a person wrote it to another person.

**4. They perform sincerity.**
"[mention a recent project they did]" — the bracket is still there. It is a template pretending to be a letter. The reader sees it immediately. The trust is gone before the second sentence.

**The diagnosis:** These emails are *describing the shape of an idea* rather than *being inside one*. Lots of words moving toward a meaning that never arrives. Weight without substance — the opposite of Calvino's lightness.

### The Cover Letter Progression

This is a real drafting sequence. The task was to write one paragraph about working at Aesop in a way that connected to Sunday Salon — an independent Melbourne art platform — without making the connection explicit.

**Draft 1**
> *"Melbourne's creative world is tightly connected, and working inside one of its more culturally embedded businesses gave me a genuine feel for it. I imagine it's a sensibility Sunday Salon knows well."*

**What went wrong:** Pre-worn phrases retrieved from general cultural discourse about Melbourne. "Tightly connected." "Culturally embedded." These could describe any city. Orwell's rule broken: metaphors seen in print before. No specificity. No vernacular. Placeless.

**Draft 2** — Gertrude Street introduced as a concrete detail.
> *"I spent two years as a UX designer at Aesop — based on Gertrude Street, which anyone who knows Melbourne understands is less a retail address than a kind of cultural node."*

**What went wrong:** The concrete detail appeared — then was immediately explained. "Which anyone who knows Melbourne understands." Calvino's lightness destroyed. The writing pointed at the meaning instead of creating the conditions for it. The reader was not trusted.

**Draft 3**
> *"long enough to understand that in this city, the most enduring creative businesses are built less on product than on the depth of the relationships around them."*

**What went wrong:** Still authoritative. The "I know something you don't" posture. Teaching rather than observing. Barthes: the writer getting in the way.

**The turn** — the writer stopped asking AI to generate and started giving AI their own words:

> *"This showed me how a business can be built through the strength of its relationships with collaborators and community."*

Raw. Unpolished. But real. Something a person who had actually lived it wrote down. Now there was material to work with.

**Draft 4:**
> *"I spent two years as a UX designer at Aesop — a formative experience that taught me how the best small businesses in Melbourne don't just operate within their community, they're sustained by it."*

**What changed:** The material changed. The writing improved not because craft was applied to the same input, but because the input became specific, true, and authored. The AI's job became editing rather than generating.

**What's still wrong:** It still over-claims. "The best small businesses in Melbourne" presents a universal judgment the writer cannot verify. It moves from lived experience to proclamation.

**Revision (epistemically humble):**
> *"I spent two years as a UX designer at Aesop — a formative experience that showed me how a small business can be sustained by its relationships with collaborators and community."*

This keeps the insight while grounding it in what the writer can honestly claim to have observed.

## Encoding This Into Prompts

### Named principles

**Principle: Epistemic Humility**

Agents should not make claims they cannot verify. Report what experience, evidence, or context showed you. Do not state universal truths unless you can substantiate them.

Use this conversion test during editing:
- If a sentence sounds like "this is how things are," rewrite it as "this is what this showed me."
- Prefer scoped claims ("in this project," "in my experience," "this suggested") over sweeping claims ("the best," "always," "everyone").
- If context is missing, ask for it or leave the claim out.

### Banned behaviours

Encode these directly into system prompts:

- Never use a metaphor, simile or figure of speech you have seen in common usage. If you have seen it before, cut it.
- Never use a long word where a short one will do.
- Never use the passive where the active will do.
- Never explain what the previous sentence already implied.
- Never start by introducing the writer. Earn that right first.
- Never name the emotional effect you want to create. Write the thing that produces it.
- Never use a bracket as a placeholder and call it personalisation.
– Never us "–"

### Structural constraints

- Write toward the meaning, not around it. The reader completes the thought.
- One idea per sentence. One purpose per paragraph.
- The first draft will always reach for the generic version of every idea. Treat it as a sketch.
- Rewrite from the most specific, concrete detail available.

### The test before publishing

Before any output is used, run it against three questions:

1. Could this sentence appear in any piece of writing on this subject? If yes, rewrite it.
2. Is there a word here that is doing no work? Cut it.
3. Does this writing know where it is — who it is for, what it is about, why now? If it could have been written for anyone, it was written for no one.

## The Workflow That Actually Works

1. **Write the raw thought yourself**, however roughly. Even a single sentence.
2. **Give AI the constraint**, not the brief. "Refine this without explaining it" is a better instruction than "write something warm about community."
3. **Push back on the first output.** It will be the generic version. That is not a failure — it is the starting point.
4. **Supply the specific detail.** A street name. A year. A material. A person. The more specific the input, the less generic the output.
5. **Use AI as an editor, not a writer.** The moment you ask it to generate from nothing, you get nothing dressed up as something.
