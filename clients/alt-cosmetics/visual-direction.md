# ALT. Cosmetics — Visual Direction Guide

*Reference for design decisions. Updated 19 March 2026.*

---

## The Two Worlds

ALT. lives in **intentional contrast** — the mark and the brand system pull from different places, and that tension is deliberate.

### 1. THE MARK — Abstract, Flowing, Hand-Drawn

The secondary mark should feel:
- **Abstract** — "like the Loewe where you can't really tell what it says"
- **Flowing** — organic, not rigid
- **Simple hand-drawn monogram vibes** — refined but human
- **A counterpoint** to the clinical chrome — contrast makes it interesting

**References:**
- Loewe anagram (flowing, interlocking, abstract)
- JB monogram
- SRA SRA (contained but organic)

**Avoid:**
- Overly structured or masculine
- Anything too old or material-based (no ancient textures, primitive marks, carved stone, Victorian flourishes)
- Competing with the chrome — it should complement, not dominate

### 2. THE BRAND SYSTEM — Clean, Clinical, Chrome

Everything else (typography, packaging, photography, tone) should feel:
- **Medical precision** meets **object design**
- Chrome tubes like a Braun product or Dieter Rams object
- **Minimal typography** — Aktiv Grotesk Extended, geometric, confident
- Product as **"object for the home"** — displayed, not hidden
- White/grey on chrome — low contrast, restrained

**References:**
- Acne Studios (fashion/editorial, bold but restrained)
- Prada Beauty (object worship, premium)
- Nolew (bold, metallic, graphic)
- Byredo (intimate, object-like)

**Avoid:**
- Aesop territory (too ritual, too warm, too earthy)
- GrandeLash territory (too mass, too functional)
- The Ordinary (too clinical-cold, too budget)
- Wellness clichés (ritual, glow, nourishing)

## 3. VISUAL LANGUAGE — Scientific Naturalism *(added 20 Mar 2026)*

The brand's plant-based story lives in the *context* around the product — not in the mark, not in decorative elements, but in photography, texture, and background imagery.

**The concept:** ALT. treats its ingredients the way a scientist treats something precious — observed closely, named precisely, held up to the light. This is the "whisper don't shout" made visual.

**What this looks like:**
- Botanical specimens on white ground — cross-sections, pressed flora, macro photography
- Microscopy: plant cell structure, translucent leaves, backlit organism slides
- Ingredient imagery — raw material, not processed/packaged
- Scientific illustration register — precise line, no sentimentality
- 90s pharmaceutical design typography (not Aesop-warm — colder, more precise)

**What this is NOT:**
- Aesop (explicitly avoided — too ritual, too earthy, too warm)
- Decorative botanical (florals, wreaths, ornamental illustration)
- Wellness clichés (soft light, linen, hands holding bottles)

**Reference sources:**
- [Biodiversity Heritage Library](https://www.biodiversitylibrary.org/subject/Botanical+illustrations) — digitised natural history journals, specimen labels, taxonomic typography
- [Biodiversity Heritage Library — Nomenclature](https://www.biodiversitylibrary.org/subject/Nomenclature) — scientific naming conventions, letterform register
- [Wellcome Collection — Specimens](https://wellcomecollection.org/works/tzxt6jzc) — medical/scientific imagery archive, pharmaceutical ephemera
- [Wellcome Collection](https://wellcomecollection.org/collections) — 35,000+ items of pharmaceutical and medical product literature
- Ernst Haeckel, *Kunstformen der Natur* — structural geometry of organisms, radial symmetry, microscopic life forms (search: "Haeckel art forms nature plates")
- [Aesop — Fonts In Use](https://fontsinuse.com/uses/20234/aesop-logo-website-and-packaging) — Optima/Zapf Humanist, scientific-apothecary typographic register. Reference the *type logic*, not the brand aesthetic.
- [Aesop bespoke typeface — Behance](https://www.behance.net/gallery/91617815/Aesop-Bespoke-Typeface-Design) — how scientific precision translates to letterform

## The Contrast Principle

The magic is in the **tension**:

| Element | Character |
|---------|-----------|
| **Mark** | Organic, flowing, abstract, hand-drawn feeling |
| **Wordmark** | Geometric, hard, extended, mechanical |
| **Packaging** | Chrome, clinical, precision-engineered |
| **Product experience** | Object for the home, considered, special |

They shouldn't look like they came from the same place — that's intentional.

---

## Quick Checklist

When evaluating design work, ask:

**For the mark:**
- [ ] Is it abstract enough that you can't immediately read it?
- [ ] Does it feel flowing/organic, not rigid?
- [ ] Is it simple and refined, not overworked?
- [ ] Does it contrast nicely with the geometric wordmark?

**For everything else:**
- [ ] Does it feel clinical/premium, not warm/earthy?
- [ ] Would it look at home in a design museum gift shop?
- [ ] Is typography restrained and confident?
- [ ] Does it whisper rather than shout?
